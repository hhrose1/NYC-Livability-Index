How to Check HPD Violations for NYC Apartments

Step-by-Step Building Investigation

1

Search for Violations

Go to HPD Online and check three tabs:

  • Complaints
  • Violations
  • Bedbug Reports
2

Assess Violation Severity

Review violations by class (A/B/C) and check:

  • How many are open vs. closed?
  • How recent are they?
  • What codes are they?
3

Something Looks Suspicious

If you see no violations at all or something seems off:

  • Look up the lot and block number in the NYC Open Data HPD Violations dataset
  • Cross-reference to catch missing or unreported violations
4

Research the Landlord

Look up the owner in the HPD database, then:

  • Google the owner's name — are there lawsuits? News articles?
  • Google the property management company — check reviews and complaints
  • Read what people actually say, not just star ratings:
    • ❌ "He didn't approve my application" = normal business
    • 🚩 "I had no hot water for 8 months" = major red flag

Understanding HPD Violations: Key Tips

📋 Decoding Violation Codes

  • The HPD code is the number directly in front of the § symbol
  • Search codes in the table below

🔍 Reading Between the Lines

  • Check the Complaints tab — multiple complaints but no violations could mean overly thorough neighbors reporting minor issues to force landlord action
  • Age matters more than quantity — a string of violations from 2001 matters much less than a single closed Class C violation in 2025

⚠️ Violation Severity Guide

  • Class A (minor): A few Class A violations are normal for older buildings
  • Class B (hazardous): Multiple open Class B violations = yellow flag
  • Class C (immediately hazardous): ANY open Class C violations = red flag

🚨 Critical Red Flag Codes

  • 729/730 (AEP/Underlying Conditions): HPD has flagged the building as chronically neglected
  • 759/760/765: Apartments ordered vacated — if someone's renting it anyway, that's illegal and dangerous

📊 Violation Status

  • Open violation: Not yet fixed
  • Closed violation: Already fixed
